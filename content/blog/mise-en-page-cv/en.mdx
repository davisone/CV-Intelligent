A resume can be brilliantly written and still get rejected because of poor layout. Conversely, a polished design will not save empty content. The reality is that both substance and presentation matter — and in the first few seconds of review, it is the presentation that creates the impression. Recruiters spend an average of 6 to 7 seconds on an initial visual scan. Your layout determines whether those seconds make them want to read further.

## The Golden Rule: Readability Before Aesthetics

Before any visual flourish, your resume must be **easy to read and easy to scan**. A recruiter does not read a resume from top to bottom on the first pass — they scan it diagonally, looking for visual anchors: section headings, company names, job titles, degrees, dates.

Good layout facilitates this visual journey. Poor layout slows it down or blocks it entirely.

**ATS compatibility is the second imperative.** Applicant Tracking Systems are software tools that automatically parse your resume before a human ever sees it. They extract text, search for keywords, and assign a relevance score. A visually complex resume — with tables, text boxes, or advanced graphic elements — can be parsed incorrectly or rendered completely unreadable by an ATS. The result: your application is filtered out before it reaches a person.

Human readability and ATS compatibility point in the same direction: **clarity, structure, simplicity**.

## Choosing the Right Font

Font choice matters more than most candidates realize. It affects readability, perceived professionalism, and compatibility with different reading systems.

**Recommended fonts:**

- **Arial**: clean, modern, neutral. Excellent readability on screen and in print. A safe choice for any industry.
- **Calibri**: Microsoft Word's default since 2007. Contemporary, well-spaced, broadly well-received.
- **Georgia**: an elegant serif font, well-suited to traditional sectors (law, finance, consulting).
- **Garamond**: refined and understated, ideal for academic profiles, literary fields, or luxury industry applications.
- **Helvetica / Helvetica Neue**: minimalist and modern, widely used in creative and tech sectors.

**Recommended sizes:**
- Body text: **10 to 12 pt** (11 pt is often the best compromise)
- Section headings: **13 to 16 pt**
- Your name in the header: **18 to 22 pt**

**Fonts to avoid entirely:**
- **Times New Roman**: dated, associated with 1990s Word documents
- **Comic Sans**: unprofessional, never appropriate on a formal document
- **Courier**: typewriter connotation, out of context for most applications
- Handwriting or decorative fonts: illegible and incompatible with ATS

**Core rule**: use only one font family throughout your resume. You can create visual hierarchy using weight (regular, **bold**) and style (italic) without introducing multiple typefaces.

## Margins: Framing and Breathing Room

Margins define the white space surrounding your content. Too narrow, and the document feels suffocating. Too wide, and you waste valuable space on an A4 or letter-sized page.

**Recommended margins: 0.75 to 1 inch (approximately 2 to 2.5 cm) on all sides.** This is the standard range that balances readability with information density.

**Never go below 0.5 inches (1.25 cm).** Below this threshold, the document becomes hard to read and content may be cut off by some printers.

Conversely, do not exceed 1.25 inches if you have content to fit on one page. Overly generous margins can push relevant content onto a second page unnecessarily.

## Spacing and Line Height

The spacing between lines and between sections is what gives your resume air. A document that is too dense is visually off-putting and difficult to scan.

**Recommended line spacing: 1.15 to 1.5**

- **1.0** (single spacing): too tight, creates an oppressive density
- **1.15**: a good readability-to-density compromise, recommended for profiles with extensive experience
- **1.5**: open and comfortable, ideal for junior profiles with less content to fill the page
- **2.0** (double spacing): too open, suggests a lack of content, appears unprofessional

**Section spacing**: add slightly more vertical space between major sections (Experience, Education, Skills) than between items within a section. This visual hierarchy helps the eye navigate quickly.

## One-Column vs. Two-Column Layout

Column structure is one of the most consequential layout decisions — and it has direct implications for ATS compatibility.

### One-Column Resume

**Advantages:**
- Natural linear reading flow, top to bottom
- Maximum ATS compatibility: text is extracted in logical order
- Universally understood structure

**Disadvantages:**
- Can appear austere without careful design work
- Lower visual density

### Two-Column Resume

**Advantages:**
- Visually attractive and modern
- Allows more information to fit on one page
- Useful for separating contact/skills information from work history

**Major disadvantages:**
- **Critical ATS problem**: most ATS parsers read documents left to right, line by line across the full width. A two-column resume is frequently read in the wrong order, merging content from both columns into a jumbled mix. The result is an incoherent extraction and a collapsed ATS score.
- Less suited for applications at large companies with formal, automated recruitment processes

**Practical recommendation**: if you are submitting your resume through an online application portal or a company careers page, use a single column. If you are sending your resume directly to a human recruiter by email, a two-column layout can work.

## Color on a Resume

Color is a powerful tool that demands restraint on a professional resume.

**The resume color rule: one accent color maximum, used sparingly.**

This accent color can be applied to:
- Section headings
- A thin dividing line or rule
- Your name in the header
- Contact icons

**By industry:**

- **Conservative sectors** (finance, law, government, banking): black and white only, or at most a dark navy blue or charcoal grey. Any bright color is inappropriate.
- **Mid-range sectors** (marketing, sales, project management): a moderate accent color is acceptable and can help your resume stand out from the pile.
- **Creative sectors** (design, advertising, media, fashion): more freedom is permitted, but readability remains the priority. An over-designed resume is still counterproductive.

**Non-negotiable contrast rules:**
- Dark text (black or very dark grey) on white or very light background
- Never light text on a light background (e.g., light grey on white)
- Never light text on a bright colored background (illegible when printed)

## Graphic Elements

Icons, dividers, skill bars, and other graphic elements can enhance the visual appeal of a resume — but they carry significant risks.

**What works:**
- **Simple contact icons** (phone, email, LinkedIn, location): they improve the readability and quick identification of contact information. Used sparingly, they are tolerated by most ATS systems.
- **Thin section dividers**: subtle horizontal rules between sections help structure the document without visual clutter.

**What causes problems:**
- **Skill bars** (e.g., JavaScript ●●●●○): visually appealing but conceptually flawed. How do you objectively quantify CSS proficiency on a 5-point scale? Experienced recruiters often view these elements skeptically. They are also invisible to ATS systems, meaning any skill you only represent with a bar may not be extracted.
- **Infographics and charts**: completely unreadable for ATS and difficult for humans to interpret at a glance.
- **Company logos and embedded images**: increase file size and create parsing problems.

## Section Order

The order of your resume sections should match your profile and your goal.

**Entry-level profile (under 3 years of experience):**
1. Header (name, contact information, photo if applicable)
2. Professional summary (optional but recommended)
3. Education
4. Experience (internships, part-time work, student projects)
5. Skills
6. Interests (if relevant)

**Senior profile (5+ years of experience):**
1. Header
2. Professional summary
3. Work experience (first — this is your primary asset)
4. Skills
5. Education
6. Certifications (if relevant)

**Career change profile:**
1. Header
2. Targeted summary focused on the new field
3. Transferable skills (highlighted prominently)
4. Work experience (recontextualized toward the new direction)
5. Recent training and certifications in the new field
6. Original education

## Layout Tools

### Microsoft Word
**Advantages**: universal, excellent for simple templates, .docx files are widely compatible.
**Pitfalls**: complex layouts (columns, tables) can shift depending on the recipient's Word version. .docx files are sometimes poorly parsed by ATS.

### Google Docs
**Advantages**: accessible online, easy sharing, direct PDF export.
**Pitfalls**: limited design options, complex layouts harder to maintain consistently.

### Canva
**Advantages**: visually striking results, large template library.
**Major pitfalls**: PDFs generated by Canva are often built from images rather than selectable text. An ATS cannot extract anything from them. Canva resumes are only appropriate for direct human delivery — never for online application forms.
