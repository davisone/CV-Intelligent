The skills section of your resume is often the first thing an ATS algorithm reads — and among the first things a human recruiter scans. Yet it's also one of the most poorly written sections: too vague, too long, or simply missing. Knowing what to include, how to organize it, and at what level of detail is what separates a resume that clears the automated filter from one that ends up in the trash.

In this article, we'll break down how to build a strong skills section, with over 100 examples organized by industry to help you find the right words.

## Hard Skills vs Soft Skills: Understanding the Difference

Before writing anything, you need to understand the fundamental distinction between these two broad categories.

**Hard skills** (technical skills) are measurable, learned through training or experience. They can be verified and tested. Examples: Python proficiency, financial accounting, arc welding, Scrum project management, foreign language fluency, ERP system operation.

**Soft skills** (behavioral or interpersonal skills) are harder to quantify. They describe how you work rather than what you technically know how to do. Examples: communication, leadership, adaptability, problem-solving, stress management.

Both belong on a resume — but not in the same place, and not presented the same way. Hard skills get listed. Soft skills get proven: they're best illustrated in your experience descriptions ("led a team of 5 people") rather than in a bare list.

## How to Structure Your Skills Section

There's no single right way to present your skills, but some structures work better than others depending on your profile.

**Categorized list format**
This is the most readable format for both recruiters and ATS systems. Group your skills by theme:
- Languages and frameworks
- Tools and software
- Methodologies
- Languages spoken

**Progress bar format**
Visually appealing, but use with caution. Skill levels are subjective, and experienced recruiters know that "90% in Excel" doesn't mean much. Prefer clear qualitative levels: Beginner, Intermediate, Advanced, Expert.

**Order of appearance**
Always lead with the skills most relevant to the target role. A recruiter scanning your resume in 7 seconds needs to see the most important skills first.

**Language skills**
Always specify your level using a recognized framework (CEFR: A1 to C2, or standardized test scores such as TOEFL, IELTS, DELF). "Fluent English" is vague; "English C1 — TOEFL 105" is factual and credible.

## How to Honestly Indicate Your Skill Level

Indicating a proficiency level can strengthen your credibility — as long as you're honest. Lying about skills gets discovered quickly during technical interviews or in the first weeks on the job.

Here is a simple, honest rating scale:

- **Basic / Foundational**: You've been exposed to the skill and understand the fundamentals, but cannot use it independently yet
- **Intermediate**: You can use it autonomously on standard tasks, but still have gaps in advanced areas
- **Advanced**: You handle most use cases including complex ones, and can guide less experienced colleagues
- **Expert**: You have deep mastery, are an internal reference, and actively follow developments in the field

For languages, use the CEFR framework (A1 to C2) or cite an official test score (TOEFL, IELTS, DALF, DELE, HSK...).

## Skills You Should NOT Put on Your Resume

Some skills are so basic or universal that they add no value to your application.

**Avoid:**
- "Proficient in Word, Excel, PowerPoint" — unless the role explicitly requires it and you have an advanced level to substantiate
- "Internet" — this says nothing
- "Punctuality", "politeness", "sense of responsibility" — these are minimum expectations, not differentiators
- "Professional appearance" — goes without saying
- Skills you don't actually have — dishonesty gets uncovered
- Skills completely unrelated to the role — they take up space and dilute your profile

Resume space is limited. Every line needs to earn its place.
