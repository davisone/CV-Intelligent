You spent hours crafting your resume. You applied to dozens of jobs. And yet — silence. No callbacks, no interviews, nothing. Sound familiar?

In most cases, the problem is not your experience or qualifications. It is that your resume never made it past the first gatekeeper: the **Applicant Tracking System (ATS)**.

Understanding how ATS software works — and how to write a resume that passes its filters — is one of the most important skills a job seeker can develop in 2025.

## What Is an ATS and How Does It Work?

An Applicant Tracking System is software used by recruiters and HR departments to collect, sort, and filter job applications. Instead of reading every resume manually, companies rely on ATS platforms like Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, or Taleo to do the first round of screening.

The ATS scans your resume for specific keywords, formats, and structures. If your resume does not match the criteria set by the recruiter, it is rejected — often before a human ever reads it. Studies suggest that **over 75% of resumes are discarded by ATS before reaching a recruiter**.

The good news: once you know the rules, you can work with them.

## File Format Mistakes That Get You Rejected Immediately

One of the most common reasons resumes fail ATS parsing is using the wrong file format. Here is what you need to know:

- **Use .docx or a clean PDF.** Most modern ATS systems handle both, but some older systems struggle with PDFs. When in doubt, submit a .docx file unless the job posting specifies otherwise.
- **Avoid image-based PDFs.** If your resume was created as a design file and exported as a flat image, the ATS cannot read the text at all.
- **No headers or footers for important information.** Many ATS parsers ignore text inside headers and footers. Keep your name, phone number, and email in the main body of the document.
- **Skip the columns.** Two-column layouts look great to the human eye but confuse many ATS parsers, causing your experience and skills to be read out of order or merged together.

## Keyword Optimization: The Core of ATS Success

ATS systems rank resumes based on how well they match the job description. Your job is to mirror the language used in the posting — but naturally, not robotically.

**How to identify the right keywords:**

1. Read the job description carefully and highlight repeated words, especially job titles, required skills, and tools.
2. Look at the "requirements" and "responsibilities" sections — these contain the highest-weight terms.
3. Note both long-form and abbreviated versions (for example, "Search Engine Optimization" and "SEO").

**How to integrate keywords:**

- Work them into your bullet points in context. Do not just list them at the bottom of your resume in white text (ATS systems are now smart enough to catch that trick).
- Match the exact phrasing when possible. If the job description says "project management," use that phrase rather than "project oversight."
- Use keywords in your summary, experience, and skills sections — not just one place.

## Use Standard Section Headers

ATS software is trained to recognize specific section titles. If you rename them creatively, the system may fail to categorize your information correctly.

**Use these standard headers:**
- Work Experience (not "My Journey" or "Career Highlights")
- Education (not "Academic Background")
- Skills (not "Tools I Know" or "My Toolkit")
- Certifications
- Projects
- Volunteer Experience

Sticking to these standard labels ensures the ATS correctly maps your content to the right fields.

## ATS-Friendly Formatting Principles

Beyond file format and keywords, the visual structure of your resume affects parsing quality significantly.

- **Use a single-column layout.** Clean, linear, top-to-bottom.
- **Avoid tables, text boxes, and graphics.** These elements are often skipped or scrambled by ATS parsers.
- **Stick to standard fonts.** Arial, Calibri, Garamond, and Times New Roman are universally readable. Decorative or custom fonts may not render correctly.
- **Use standard bullet points.** Simple dashes or round bullets work best. Avoid custom symbols or icons.
- **Keep consistent spacing.** Use standard margins (ideally 0.75 to 1 inch on all sides).

## Testing Your Resume Before Submitting

Before you apply, do a quick ATS simulation on your resume:

1. **Copy and paste your resume text into a plain text editor** (like Notepad). If the content looks garbled, mixed up, or unreadable, an ATS will have the same problem.
2. **Use an ATS checker tool** like Jobscan or Resume Worded to compare your resume against a specific job description.
3. **Check that all key qualifications from the posting appear somewhere in your resume** — ideally in the experience or skills section.

This simple test can reveal formatting problems you would never notice in a visual preview.

## What Comes After the ATS

Passing the ATS is only the first step. Once a human recruiter opens your resume, it needs to be clear, concise, and compelling within about six seconds — the average time spent on an initial scan.

That means your resume must work on two levels: machine-readable for the ATS, and human-readable for the recruiter. Fortunately, the same principles apply: clean formatting, clear structure, and relevant content.
