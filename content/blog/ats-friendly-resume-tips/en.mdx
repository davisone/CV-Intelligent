Most job applications never reach a human recruiter. They are filtered out automatically by Applicant Tracking Systems — software that scans, scores, and sorts resumes before a person ever sees them. If your resume is not optimized for these systems, even excellent qualifications will not save you from the reject pile.

The good news is that making your resume ATS-friendly is straightforward once you know what to do. Here are seven tips that will immediately improve your chances.

## 1. Use Keywords Directly from the Job Posting

ATS software scores your resume by comparing its content against the job description. The more your resume matches the exact language used in the posting, the higher your score.

Read the job description carefully. Identify the most frequently mentioned skills, tools, job titles, and qualifications. Then make sure those exact terms appear in your resume — in your summary, your experience section, and your skills list.

For example, if the job description mentions "cross-functional collaboration" three times, that phrase should appear somewhere in your resume. Do not paraphrase it as "working with different departments." The ATS is matching strings, not interpreting meaning.

**Practical step:** Copy the job description into a word frequency tool or simply read it twice and highlight every technical term and qualification. Check each one against your resume.

## 2. Stick to a Single-Column Layout

Two-column layouts are visually appealing in a portfolio or a creative field context, but they create serious problems for ATS parsing. Most ATS systems read documents left to right, top to bottom, in a single stream. A two-column layout often causes text from separate columns to be merged, creating garbled, unreadable output.

A one-column, top-to-bottom layout eliminates this risk entirely. Your experience, education, and skills will be parsed in the correct order, and the ATS will categorize them correctly.

Keep design simple: consistent margins, clean typography, and clear section separations using bold headers — no fancy dividers or sidebars.

## 3. Use Standard Section Headers

ATS systems are trained on thousands of resumes and learn to recognize specific section labels. If you use non-standard headers, the system may fail to correctly categorize your content.

**Use these headers:**
- Work Experience (or Professional Experience)
- Education
- Skills
- Certifications
- Projects
- Volunteer Experience
- Languages

**Avoid creative alternatives** like "My Story," "What I Bring," or "Skills & Superpowers." These may work on a personal website, but they will confuse an ATS and cause your data to be misclassified or ignored.

## 4. Submit in the Correct File Format

When a job application form asks for a resume upload, the file format matters. Here is the general rule:

- **DOCX is the safest universal format.** Almost every ATS parses it correctly.
- **PDF is fine in most modern systems**, but can cause issues with older ATS software or when the PDF contains complex formatting, tables, or embedded images.
- **Never submit an image file** (JPG, PNG, or a scanned document). ATS systems cannot read text from images.

When in doubt, upload DOCX. If the system specifically requests PDF, provide a clean, text-based PDF — not an image export.

## 5. Avoid Tables, Text Boxes, and Graphics

Tables, text boxes, and graphic elements (icons, charts, infographics) are visually attractive but structurally invisible to most ATS parsers. The text inside them is often skipped entirely or output as unstructured noise.

This is critical: if you list your skills inside a table or a styled box, the ATS may not read those skills at all. You could have every keyword the recruiter is looking for — and still fail the automated screening.

Replace any tables with plain bulleted lists. Move all content out of text boxes and into the main body of the document. Remove icons next to section headers. The result will look plainer but will perform dramatically better in ATS systems.

## 6. Use Standard, Widely-Supported Fonts

Unusual fonts can cause rendering issues in ATS software. Stick to fonts that are universally supported and easy to parse:

- Arial
- Calibri
- Garamond
- Times New Roman
- Helvetica

Font size should be between 10 and 12 points for body text, and 14 to 16 points for your name. Avoid very small text — if you are trying to fit everything on one page by shrinking the font to 9pt, that is a sign you need to cut content instead.

## 7. Test Your Resume with a Plain Text Copy-Paste

Before submitting any application, run a quick self-test. Copy your entire resume and paste it into a plain text editor (Notepad on Windows, TextEdit in plain text mode on Mac). Then look at the result.

If you see:
- Text running together or merging from different sections
- Bullet points turning into strange characters
- Section headers not standing out from body text
- Skills or experience appearing in the wrong order

...then an ATS will have the same problems. Fix the underlying formatting issues until the plain text version is clean, logical, and readable from top to bottom.

This five-minute test is one of the best quality checks you can run on your resume before applying.
